Conclusion

What we did!

We had set out with one goal in mind. To find out who could, in the near future, join the Basketball Hall of Fame by examining players in the NBA. After cleaning up the data, we created two simple models to determine who could join, both of which can be found in the bottom section of our code page. The first model took into account who, using statistics taken in 2017, could have already joined the Hall of Fame given accomplishments completed. The second model looked for performance on a per game or per time basis rather that overall achievements to see who could join the Hall of Fame in the future. The results show that although many known names are set to fit straight into the Hall of Fame, names that gained popularity after 2017 show up in the second model!

Considerations We Took

Perhaps most important was our decision to go with a random forest. Perhaps we should have swapped away from random forest as went deeper in but initially, we had been working with many seperate variables so the best way forward was with a random forest. When we finally did decide to focus down specific variables, we chose to remove variables that would be clearly dependent on other variables. For example, Player Effeciciency Rating was already a combination of other variables so using it along with the other variables would only confuse the function more. In the end, we decided to look at overall performance and per game/time performace to judge both who had already had the largest impact on the game and who could become a major impact in the near future.

Was it perfect?

Our actual model is truly far from perfect. First, we did do some data erasing due to unknown values. Additionally, it can be said that the size of our data set is actually quite small. From our Kaggle data, we found more then 4000 players. However, of all of the people in the Basketball Hall of Fame, only 177 of them were players and out of the 177, only 110 were from the NBA. If you take into account the fact we split the set yet again in order to test our model, the amount of Hall of Fame players in both the training set and testing set were quite small in number. Additionally, it is important to note that our data we used to create our model was composed of data from 1977-1997 and the data we were experimenting with was data from 1997-2017. Over the course of those years the game had changed. However, using anyone who has retired in the most recent 20 years or are still in the game for training the model would also be unfair because it is still possible for those players to be inducted into the Hall of Fame.
